**Best Leads**
- The best-fit literature is still Kevin Ford’s divisor-interval work, especially [The distribution of integers with a divisor in a given interval](https://annals.math.princeton.edu/2008/168-2/p01). This is the cleanest outside framework for a packing/realizability theorem of the form “how much small-prime blocking can a family of upper-half moves actually realize?”
- A very plausible companion is Ford’s [Rough integers with a divisor in a given interval](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.02548). If your obstruction really depends on excluding low-prime contamination in carriers, this is closer than the generic Annals paper.
- The Round 14/15 upper-bound ideas look methodologically close to probabilistic anatomy papers, especially Ford’s [Joint Poisson distribution of prime factors in sets](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.12650), plus divisor/factorization distribution papers like [Distribution Laws of Smooth Divisors](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.05955) and [Dirichlet law for factorization of integers, polynomials and permutations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.14728). I do not think these solve the game directly, but they look like the right language for turning your piecewise density heuristics into a rigorous comparison sequence.
- For static geometry, [McNew on the divisor graph](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.04923), [Melotti–Saias on path partitions](https://arxiv.org/abs/1807.07783), and Saias’s newer divisor-graph work such as [Etude du graphe divisoriel 6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.12056) are relevant. My take is that these are better for structure/compression/decomposition than for a full game solution by themselves.
- [Lichtman’s proof of the Erdős primitive set conjecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.02384) is probably the most relevant primitive-set paper. It feels useful for weighted antichain inequalities and local-to-global sieve ideas, but less obviously for the dynamic game core.
- [Positional Games](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-0348-0825-5) and saturation-game papers like [On saturation games](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0195669815001341) are good methodological references, but I would treat them as packaging help, not the missing number-theoretic ingredient.

My strongest inference is that the most promising route to a full solution is now Shortener-side and sublinear-looking, not Prolonger-side linear via the current fan hierarchy. Concretely: either prove the Ford-style packing/realizability theorem your notes already point to, or turn the Round 14/15 density picture into a rigorous monotone model-sequence argument.
