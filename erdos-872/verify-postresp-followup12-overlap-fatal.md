Here is an adversarial audit of the proposed theorem and its supporting arguments.The short summary: Theorem 5 is logically sound as stated, and it successfully neutralizes the specific "universal block-product counter" described in the literature. However, the "disjoint support" condition is a massive crutch. The "overlapping carriers" counterexample is not a minor edge case; it is a fatal structural obstruction that Shortener cannot easily patch.Here is the line-by-line verification of the specific areas you asked me to check.1. Phase-2 Legality and Resolution ($p^{e(p)+1}$)Verdict: Complete and sound.Legality: Shortener plays $p^{e(p)+1}$. Is it an antichain?Against Phase 1 primes: $p$ was not played (it was illegal because $p \mid C(p)$). For any other prime $s$, $s \nmid p^{e(p)+1}$ and $p^{e(p)+1} \nmid s$.Against $C(p)$: $C(p)$ has $\ge 2$ distinct primes, so it contains some $q \neq p$. Thus $C(p) \nmid p^{e(p)+1}$, and $p^{e(p)+1} \nmid C(p)$ because the exponent $e(p)+1 > e(p)$.Against other carriers $C(q)$: Because supports are disjoint, $p \nmid C(q)$, so they are mutually indivisible.The $p^{e(p)+1} > n$ case: If the power exceeds $n$, Shortener simply skips playing it. Does the resolution argument still hold? Yes. If a surviving $x$ has $r^{e(r)+1} \mid x$, and $r^{e(r)+1} > n$, then $x \ge r^{e(r)+1} > n$. But the game is played on $\{2, \dots, n\}$. So no such $x$ can exist in the valid game space. The move is skipped because it's not needed.2. Phase-3 Legality (Cross-carrier semiprimes $pq$)Verdict: Complete and sound (under the disjoint assumption).Legality: The argument claims no carrier contains both $p$ and $q$. Because the supports of $C(p)$ and $C(q)$ are pairwise disjoint, $C(p)$ is the only composite Prolonger move containing $p$, and $C(q)$ is the only one containing $q$. Therefore, no Prolonger move contains $pq$, meaning $pq \nmid C$ for all carriers $C$.Could a carrier $C \mid pq$? Since carriers are composites, $C$ would have to be exactly $pq$. But that would mean $C$ contains both $p$ and $q$, violating disjointness.Pure prime powers: If $C(p) = p^a$, then $pq$ cannot be divided by $p^a$ (since $a \ge 2$, else it's not composite). And $pq \nmid p^a$ because $q \neq p$. So pure prime powers don't break legality.3. The Resolution ArgumentVerdict: Sound, but relies on a hidden (though true) implication about Phase 1.Subcase 1 ($x \mid C(p)$): If $x$ divides $C(p)$, then $x$ is comparable to a Prolonger move, making it illegal. Handled correctly.Subcase 2 ($r^{e(r)+1} \mid x$): If $x$ does not divide $C(p)$, since $x$ only contains primes from $C(p)$, at least one prime $r \in C(p)$ must appear in $x$ with a strictly greater exponent than it has in $C(p)$. Thus $r^{e(r)+1} \mid x$. Handled correctly.The Hidden Assumption: The proof casually states, "All primes of $x$ in $B$ (else some prime outside $B$ appears, but phase 1 played it, contradiction)." This assumes Phase 1 successfully played every prime $> y$. Is that true? Yes, because the theorem explicitly assumes all Prolonger composites have prime factors $\le y$. Therefore, no prime $> y$ divides a Prolonger move, making every prime $> y$ legal for Shortener to play. The logic holds, but the reliance on the $\le y$ cap should be explicitly flagged.4. Inclusion of the Universal Block-Product CounterVerdict: Matches perfectly.The universal counter partitions primes $\le n^{1/3}$ into disjoint sets $S_i$ and plays $x_i = \prod_{p \in S_i} p$.Since $S_i$ are disjoint partitions, the prime supports of $x_i$ are pairwise disjoint.Since $y = n^\alpha$ with $\alpha > 1/3$, all primes in the universal counter are $\le n^{1/3} < y$.Therefore, the universal block-product counter is strictly a subset of the "disjoint small-prime carrier" class. Theorem 5 legitimately defeats it.5. Arithmetic Move Count ($\pi(y)^2$)Verdict: Rigorously correct.For $y = n^\alpha$ with $\alpha < 1/2$, the number of pairs is bounded by $\pi(y)^2 \approx (y/\log y)^2 \approx n^{2\alpha} / (\log n)^2$.Because $\alpha < 1/2$, we have $2\alpha < 1$.Any power $n^c$ with $c < 1$ is strictly $o(n/\log n)$. This elegantly bypasses the $O(k^2)$ explosion that plagues prefix-Shortener strategies that play too many primes.6. The "Overlapping Carriers" Counterexample: Is it a genuine obstruction?Verdict: It is a devastating, genuine obstruction. The disjointness assumption is not just a simplification; it is the only thing keeping the Shortener strategy alive.The researcher asks if a "slightly stronger Shortener strategy" can handle the $\{30, 42, 70\}$ leaving $105$ obstruction. The answer is no, not without a catastrophic explosion in move count.Here is the adversarial breakdown of why overlapping carriers break the proof entirely:Omitted-Vertex Shadowing is Absolute: As shown in the prompt's Lemma 3, if Prolonger plays $30, 42, 70$, Shortener cannot play any of the semiprimes $15, 21, 35$ because they each divide a Prolonger carrier. Shortener's Phase 3 is completely locked out.Forced into $\Omega \ge 3$: To kill the surviving $105 = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7$, Shortener must play $105$ itself (or a higher multiple).Combinatorial Capacity limits: Can Prolonger actually scale this up to ruin the whole board? Yes. Prolonger has roughly $n/\log n$ moves. A Prolonger move $M \le n$ can have up to $\sim \log_2 n$ distinct prime factors.Each such move covers $\binom{\log_2 n}{2} \approx \frac{1}{2}(\log_2 n)^2$ pairs of primes.Over $\sim n/\log n$ moves, Prolonger can cover $\approx n \log n$ pairs of primes.The total number of pairs of primes $\le y = n^{0.45}$ is only $\approx \pi(n^{0.45})^2 \approx n^{0.9}$.Because $n \log n \gg n^{0.9}$, Prolonger has vastly more capacity than necessary to ensure that every single pair of primes $\le y$ is covered by at least one carrier.The Conclusion on Overlapping Carriers:If Prolonger abandons the "disjoint" universal counter and instead plays a dense hypergraph of overlapping primes (e.g., covering all pairs), Shortener's Phase 3 cannot play any semiprimes. Shortener is forced to play triples ($pqr$). But the number of triples $\le y$ is $\approx \pi(y)^3 \approx n^{3\alpha}$.If $\alpha = 0.45$, $n^{3\alpha} = n^{1.35}$, which is greater than $n$. Shortener would need more moves than the entire game board allows.Final Audit SummaryTheorem 5 is a valid, rigorous proof that the previously established "universal block-product counter" fails to force $L(n) = \Theta(n)$. However, it achieves this by restricting Prolonger to "disjoint" carriers. By doing so, it strips Prolonger of its actual optimal counter-play: overlapping prime carriers that weaponize omitted-vertex shadowing. The researcher's intuition that overlapping carriers are the true remaining gap is 100% correct, and the gap is likely too wide for this specific proof strategy to cross.
