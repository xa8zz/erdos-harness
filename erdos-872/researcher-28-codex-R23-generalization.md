# Round 28 Codex — structural characterization of the R23 certificate refutation

This note is a Codex synthesis/generalization of Round 23's smooth-squarefree counterexample. It is meant as a structural guide for the certificate-family route, not yet as a promoted theorem for `current_state.md`.

## Executive summary

The R23 refutation does **not** rely on smoothness per se. The real load-bearing input is:

1. a large squarefree support layer of size `n^{o(1)}`,
2. enough incomparable Shortener certificate moves to pair against that layer during the sweep prefix, and
3. a natural measure on the certificate family that gives non-negligible mass to the divisibility shadow generated by that layer.

That yields a fairly clean characterization.

- **Susceptible families:** any certificate family whose squarefree-support projection contains a broad Hamming layer (or downset under one) on a subpolynomial prime pool, together with an equally large incomparable witness layer. This includes the smooth-squarefree family from R23, and also exact-rank families such as `\omega(d)=k` and, with a slight modification, `\Omega(d)=k`. Threshold families such as `\tau(d)\ge K` are susceptible through their minimal squarefree rank slice.
- **Not hit by this pattern:** chain-like or otherwise support-rigid families, where there is no large incomparable witness layer and no large support shadow to saturate. The primorial family is the clean model example.
- **Middle ground:** some families are set-theoretically susceptible but measure-ambiguous. The main example is `z`-rough numbers: one can refute any Bernoulli measure concentrated on a bounded prime band `(z,y]`, but this does **not** automatically saturate the natural Buchstab/uniform measure on the full rough family.

So the honest conclusion is a **structural characterization**, not a universal "all sublinear certificates fail" theorem:

> Any certificate family with a broad squarefree support layer plus a large incomparable witness layer is vulnerable to an R23-type `n^{o(1)}` sweep-prefix attack. To evade the attack, a candidate certificate must be support-rigid enough to destroy that cube-like support geometry, or its natural measure must live far away from every such squarefree layer.

This is stronger than "downward closed families fail": downward closure is only one way to make the attack work.

## 1. The reusable template

Fix a prime pool
`P_y = \{p \text{ prime} : p \le y\}`
with `N = \pi(y)`, and a family `\mathcal F` of squarefree supports `F \subseteq P_y` of common size `k`.

For each `F \in \mathcal F`, write
`d_F := \prod_{p \in F} p`.
Assume
`d_F \le n/(2y)`, so there is a prime
`q_F \in (n/(2d_F), n/d_F)`
and hence
`x_F := d_F q_F \in (n/2, n]`.

The key point is that the `x_F`'s are automatically pairwise incomparable because they all lie in the upper half.

Now suppose `C_n \subseteq \{2,\dots,\lfloor n/2 \rfloor\}` is a certificate family, and there is an antichain
`\{s_F : F \in \mathcal F\} \subseteq C_n`
such that
`s_F \nmid x_{F'}`
for every `F,F'`.
Since `x_{F'} > n/2 > s_F`, we also have `x_{F'} \nmid s_F`.

Then the alternating prefix
`x_{F_1}, s_{F_1}, x_{F_2}, s_{F_2}, \dots`
over any ordering of `\mathcal F`
is legal.

Finally, let `T_n \subseteq C_n` be any target subfamily with the property that every `t \in T_n` divides some `x_F`. Then after the prefix, the generated certificate complex
`\mathcal K(P) := \{d \in C_n : d \mid x \text{ for some Prolonger sweep move } x\}`
contains all of `T_n`.

This gives the following template lemma.

### Template Lemma

Let `\mathcal F`, `x_F`, `s_F`, and `T_n` be as above. Then:

1. Prolonger has a legal sweep prefix of length `2|\mathcal F|`.
2. The resulting complex satisfies `T_n \subseteq \mathcal K(P)`.
3. If `|\mathcal F| = n^{o(1)}`, then the entire obstruction is generated by only `n^{o(1)}` Prolonger sweep moves.
4. For any probability measure `\nu_n` on `C_n`,
   `\nu_n(\mathcal K(P)) \ge \nu_n(T_n)`.

So the attack problem reduces to finding a large target family `T_n` inside the divisibility shadow of a subpolynomial support layer `\mathcal F`, plus enough certificate witnesses `s_F` to keep the sweep legal.

## 2. Two concrete corollaries

### Corollary A — downward-closed squarefree families

Suppose `C_n` contains every squarefree product of primes from `P_y` of support size at most `k+1`, with
`(k+1)\log y = o(\log n)`.

Take `\mathcal F = \binom{P_y}{k}`.
For each `F`, let `x_F = d_F q_F` as above.
Choose an injection from `k`-sets to `(k+1)`-sets, `F \mapsto G_F`, and put
`s_F := \prod_{p \in G_F} p`.

Then:

- the `s_F`'s form an antichain,
- no `s_F` divides any `x_{F'}` because `s_F` has `k+1` small primes while every `x_{F'}` has only `k`,
- every squarefree support `E` with `|E| \le k` lies in the shadow of some `F`,
- hence `\mathcal K(P)` contains the entire lower shadow
  `\{ \prod_{p \in E} p : E \subseteq P_y,\ 1 \le |E| \le k\}`.

This is exactly the R23 mechanism. The smooth-squarefree family is just the canonical example.

### Corollary B — exact-rank squarefree slices

Downward closure is **not** necessary.

Assume `C_n` contains every squarefree product of exactly `k` primes from `P_y`, with
`k\log y = o(\log n)`.
Assume also that membership in `C_n` is stable under replacing one small prime by one fresh large prime. Concretely, this holds for:

- `C_n = \{d : \omega(d) = k\}`,
- `C_n = \{d : \Omega(d) = k\}`,
- `C_n = \{d : \tau(d) \ge K\}` once `k` is chosen so that `2^k \ge K`.

Construction:

1. Let `\mathcal F = \binom{P_y}{k}` and define `x_F = d_F q_F` as above.
2. For each `F`, choose a distinct fresh prime
   `r_F \in (2y,\, n/(2y^{k-1})]`
   not equal to any `q_{F'}`.
3. Pick any `(k-1)`-subset `H_F \subset F`, and set
   `s_F := r_F \prod_{p \in H_F} p`.

Then:

- each `s_F` lies in the same exact-rank slice as `d_F`,
- the `s_F`'s form an antichain,
- no `s_F` divides any `x_{F'}` because `r_F` is a fresh prime not present in any `x_{F'}`,
- `d_F \mid x_F`, so the exact-rank layer itself lies in `\mathcal K(P)`.

The supply of fresh primes is enormous compared with `|\mathcal F|`:
`|\mathcal F| = \exp(O(k\log y)) = n^{o(1)}`
while
`\pi(n/(2y^{k-1})) - \pi(2y) = n^{1-o(1)}`.

So exact-rank families are vulnerable too.

## 3. Candidate-by-candidate classification

### 3.1 `C_n = \{d : \tau(d) \ge K_n\}` — susceptible through the minimal squarefree layer

Let
`r_n := \lceil \log_2 K_n \rceil`.
Every squarefree integer with exactly `r_n` prime factors lies in the certificate family, because
`\tau(d) = 2^{r_n} \ge K_n`.

Hence the exact-rank construction above applies as soon as
`r_n \log y = o(\log n)`.
It saturates the minimal squarefree layer
`\{d \text{ squarefree} : \omega(d) = r_n,\ P^+(d) \le y\}`.

What is the "natural measure"? There is no single canonical choice here. The right statement is:

> Any Bernoulli / reciprocal-mass argument whose measure gives non-negligible weight to the minimal squarefree rank-`r_n` layer is vulnerable to an R23-type refutation.

So the many-divisors family is structurally susceptible, but the strength of the negative result depends on how much of the chosen measure sits on the squarefree frontier rather than in the highly nonsquarefree tail.

### 3.2 `C_n = \{d : d \text{ primorial}\}` — immune to this pattern

This is the clean opposite extreme.

The primorials form a divisibility chain:
`2 \mid 2\cdot 3 \mid 2\cdot 3\cdot 5 \mid \cdots`.

Consequences:

1. There is no large certificate antichain `\{s_F\}` inside `C_n`; in fact, any antichain has size at most `1`.
2. The support projection is a chain of initial prime segments, not a Hamming layer.
3. For any upper-half move `x_F`, the certificate divisors of `x_F` coming from the primorial family are just the initial segments of the prime support of `x_F`, so the intersection `C_n \cap \mathcal K(P)` can grow only linearly in the support size, not exponentially in `k`.

So primorial certificates are genuinely rigid in exactly the sense the R23 attack requires.

### 3.3 `C_n = \{d \le n/2 : P^-(d) > z\}` (`z`-rough) — set-susceptible, measure-ambiguous

As a **set**, roughness does not stop the construction.

If one chooses a prime band
`z < p \le y`
and a support layer on that band, then every squarefree divisor of `x_F` built from that band is still `z`-rough. So the downward-closed attack works on the band-limited squarefree rough subfamily.

What fails is the measure statement for the **full** rough family.

The natural rough/uniform/Buchstab measure on
`\{d \le n/2 : P^-(d) > z\}`
is not concentrated on a single subpolynomial prime cube. A quick sanity check illustrates the mismatch:

- `n = 200000`,
- `z = 30`,
- `y = 200`,
- rough integers up to `n/2`: `15804`,
- squarefree band-limited integers with all prime factors in `(z,y]` and support rank `\le 1`: `36`,
- fraction: about `0.0023`.

So the correct conclusion is:

> The full rough family is structurally attackable only after **choosing a band-limited rough measure**. The R23 construction does not, by itself, refute every natural measure on the whole rough family.

### 3.4 `C_n = \{d : \Omega(d) = k\}` — susceptible

Use the exact-rank construction from Corollary B.

Take `\mathcal F = \binom{P_y}{k}` and `x_F = d_F q_F`.
The target family `T_n` is the squarefree rank-`k` slice itself:
`d_F \mid x_F`.

The certificate witnesses can be chosen as
`s_F = r_F \prod_{p \in H_F} p`
with `|H_F| = k-1` and `r_F` fresh.
Then `\Omega(s_F)=k`, so `s_F \in C_n`, and the sweep prefix is legal.

Thus any measure on the fixed-`Omega` family that gives non-negligible weight to the squarefree rank-`k` slice is vulnerable. In particular, the clean squarefree-conditioned exact-rank measure is saturated completely.

### 3.5 `C_n = \{d : \omega(d) = k\}` — susceptible, cleanest exact-rank example

This is the sharpest non-downward-closed analogue of R23.

Again choose all squarefree `k`-sets `F \subseteq P_y`, lift them to
`x_F = d_F q_F`, and take witness moves
`s_F = r_F \prod_{p \in H_F} p`
with a fresh prime `r_F`.

Then:

- `\omega(s_F) = k`,
- `\omega(d_F) = k`,
- `d_F \mid x_F`,
- no `s_F` divides any `x_{F'}`.

So the entire fixed-`omega` layer is placed in `\mathcal K(P)` using only `n^{o(1)}` Prolonger sweep moves.

This means the central-divisor family from Round 22 is not just resistant to crude incidence estimates; its exact-rank squarefree layer is **itself** R23-vulnerable.

### 3.6 `C_n = \{d \le n/2 : d \in a \pmod m\}` — generally not susceptible to the R23 mechanism

Arithmetic progressions have the wrong geometry.

The divisors of the upper-half lifts `x_F = d_F q_F` live in a multiplicative semigroup generated by the support primes. Membership in an arithmetic progression is an additive constraint with no reason to be inherited by large divisibility shadows.

So unless the progression contains a deliberately engineered multiplicative cube, the R23 mechanism has no natural target family `T_n` to saturate.

In particular:

- there is no canonical squarefree support layer inside a generic progression,
- there is no generic down-shadow inside the progression,
- and there is no natural Bernoulli measure on the progression that matches divisibility closure.

So arithmetic progressions are a natural source of **support-rigid** certificate candidates, at least with respect to this specific obstruction.

## 4. What actually blocks the construction?

The R23 pattern fails for exactly three reasons.

### A. No large certificate antichain

If `C_n` is chain-like under divisibility, then there is no large witness family `\{s_F\}`. Primorials are the model case.

### B. No large squarefree support layer

If the squarefree-support projection of `C_n` on every subpolynomial prime pool has only narrow width, then there is no large generating layer `\mathcal F`. The attack cannot even start.

### C. The natural measure lives far from every such layer

This is the rough-family phenomenon. The set may contain the needed squarefree cube, but the intended measure on the whole certificate family barely sees it.

So the search for a viable certificate should focus on families with property
`not X`, where

`X =` "contains a broad squarefree Hamming layer on some subpolynomial prime pool, together with an equally large incomparable witness layer, and the intended measure gives that layer or its shadow substantial mass."

That is the right structural replacement for the over-narrow hypothesis "downward closed."

## 5. Consequence for the certificate-family program

The main lesson is:

> Any Bernoulli / reciprocal-mass certificate argument built from a certificate family with broad squarefree support geometry is probably dead on arrival.

To survive R23-type attacks, a candidate family must be both:

1. **dynamically meaningful** enough to beat the older upper-half-lift obstruction, and
2. **support-rigid** enough to avoid the new squarefree-layer saturation mechanism.

This is a real narrowing of the search space.

- Smooth-squarefree families fail.
- Exact-rank families (`\omega=k`, `\Omega=k`) fail.
- Divisor-threshold families fail through their minimal squarefree slice unless the measure suppresses that slice.
- Primorial/chain-like families evade this attack, but may be too rigid/sparse to control residual width.
- Rough families sit in the middle: they evade the strongest measure-saturation conclusion unless one deliberately band-limits the measure.

So the most promising remaining certificate candidates are not "large natural multiplicative ideals." They are families that quotient away most of the squarefree support cube: chain-like families, canonical-representative families, or otherwise support-rigid constructions.
